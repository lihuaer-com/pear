DIY Secret Bookcase Door

Door Frame

*** Make sure to click the "View the entire album, 24 images total (14 remaining)." at the bottom of the page to see the rest of the pictures *** Ever since I was a kid I have always wanted a "Secret Door". My parents were not to keen on me building one it their home, so I had to wait until I moved out. I purchased a house earlier this year and decided I was going to finally make one! Although the door would not conceal a full blown mancave, it would however, hide the second most important room in a mans house, the porcelain throne. The bathroom was originally built "jack-and-jill" style. The bathroom has two doors, one that allows access to the master bedroom and another door going into the living room. My girlfriend and I decided the door leading from the living room to the bathroom was sort of an eye sore and awkward when people are watching TV. This is my first time working with wood at this scale, so sorry for the shoddy craftsmanship ahead of time. First thing on the agenda was creating the doors frame. If anyone attempts this project like I did, make sure your cuts and dimensions are adequate for your doors size. My door had much smaller dimensions then a standard door, so I had to do some tweaking myself with measurements to get it right. I also followed these instructions as a guide http://ana-white.com/2011/12/plans/inset-bookshelf-doorway 2 = 2" x 4" x 8' 1 = 2" x 6" x 8' (cut this do get the bottom and top piece) Screws and gorilla wood glue!

Shelving insert

Next was building the shelving insert. 3 = 1" x 6" x 8' (used to make the shelving insert.) Screws and gorilla wood glue! You may need more wood depending on how big your door is and how many shelves you want.

Door frame and shelving attached

I used screws and glue on the inside of the shelving to secure it to the frame. The proper way to do this, would be to make pocket holes around the frame and then screw in the shelving. I then sanded the whole door down for an even, smooth finish.

Test fit

Dot loved it from the get go!

The special stuff....

My girlfriend and I decided we wanted to go for an aged/rustic look with the bookshelf instead of just paint or a generic stain. We decided to oxidize the wood with a mixture of vinegar and steel wool. The tutorial used for this process can be found here: http://www.friendly-home.net/2012/07/how-to-oxidize-wood.html Basically I poured some vinegar in a jar and added some steel wool (#0000) to it. I allowed it to sit overnight and the resulting liquid looked like this. I brushed the mixture on the wood and let it dry to get a sort of oxidized/greyish/purple finish. It is important to note that this mixture reacts differently to different types of wood. The resulting color largely depends on how much "tannins" is in the wood. You can use black tea to boost the tannins levels in woods that do not have a large concentration. Doing this will make the finish come out darker. Oak, cedar, redwood, and fir have higher levels of tannins whereas pine has lower.

Dot supervising.... In this picture, you can really see how fast the oxidizing process happens. This is about 30 seconds after the mixture was applied.

This is the result after the mixture dried.

Back panel added and hinges mounted

I added some "tongue and groove" wood paneling to the back of the shelf. The paneling came from Home Depot and was in a pack of 5 if i remember correctly. I had to cut the panels and then screw them in place. The same oxidizing process was done to the paneling and as you can see, it came out lighter than the bookshelf. I even coated it first with some black tea to boost the tannins in the wood. The Hinges were also purchased at Home Depot and were "heavy duty" style door hinges. Their were 4 screw hole on each hinge. I put 2 screws into the door frame and the other 2 I used much longer screws to screw all the way into the 2x4's around the door frame. Hopefully this will prevent sagging over time. All in all, I like the finish! Now to cover up the hinges.
